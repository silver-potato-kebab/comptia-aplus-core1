OVERVIEWIntroduction

Objective 3.6 covers the power supply unit (PSU) — the component responsible for converting the alternating current (AC) coming from a wall outlet into the various direct current (DC) voltages every other component in the system actually needs to run. Every motherboard, drive, fan, and expansion card you've studied in objectives 3.3–3.5 ultimately depends on the PSU delivering clean, sufficient, correctly-voltaged power.

This objective is comparatively compact relative to 3.5, but every concept in it is directly practical: matching input voltage to regional electrical standards, understanding what each output voltage rail powers, recognizing the standard motherboard power connector, and knowing when redundancy, modularity, sufficient wattage, or energy efficiency actually matter in a real installation scenario.

AC In, DC Out — The PSU's Core Job

A wall outlet supplies AC (alternating current), where the direction of current flow reverses many times per second. Computer components require DC (direct current), where current flows in one constant direction at a steady voltage. The power supply's fundamental job is to convert AC to DC and step that voltage down to the specific, much lower voltages (3.3V, 5V, 12V) that internal components actually use — everything else in this objective builds on that core conversion function.

CONCEPT 01Input Voltage — 110–120 VAC vs. 220–240 VAC

Electrical standards differ by region of the world, and a power supply must be compatible with the voltage actually supplied by the wall outlet it's plugged into. The exam recognizes two broad input voltage standards.

StandardTypical RegionNote
110–120 VACNorth America, parts of Central/South America, JapanLower voltage, generally requires higher current (amperage) for the same wattage
220–240 VACEurope, most of Asia, Africa, Australia, much of the rest of the worldHigher voltage standard, used across the majority of the global population

Fixed-Input vs. Auto-Switching PSUs

Fixed-input (manual-switch) PSU Supports only one voltage range at a time, often via a small physical switch on the back of the unit (sometimes labeled 115/230) that must be manually set to match the local electrical standard before use. Setting this switch incorrectly for the actual input voltage can damage the power supply and potentially other connected components.
Auto-switching (wide-range) PSU Automatically detects and adapts to the input voltage across a wide range (commonly rated something like 100–240 VAC), requiring no manual switch and making the unit usable virtually anywhere in the world without configuration. Most modern consumer and business PSUs are auto-switching.

Common Pitfall

If a fixed-input power supply's voltage selector switch is set incorrectly relative to the actual wall outlet voltage — for example, set to 115V while plugged into a 230V outlet — the result can be immediate and serious hardware damage, potentially including a risk of fire. Always verify the voltage selector switch position (if present) matches the local electrical standard before powering on a system, especially after international travel/relocation or when working with imported/used hardware of unknown origin.

Exam Angle

A scenario describing a computer being shipped or relocated internationally, or a system purchased abroad and used domestically, is testing whether you know to check the PSU's input voltage compatibility — either confirming it's auto-switching, or manually setting the correct voltage range on a fixed-input unit before that system is ever powered on in its new location.

CONCEPT 02Output Voltage — 3.3V vs. 5V vs. 12V

After converting AC to DC, a PSU delivers power across several distinct voltage rails, each intended for different categories of components based on their power requirements.

Output Voltage Rails — What Each One Powers

3.3V
Motherboard chipset, some RAM circuitry
5V
USB devices, some legacy drive logic, low-power circuits
12V
CPU, GPU, drive motors, fans — the highest-power components
3.3V rail Powers lower-voltage logic circuits, including portions of the motherboard chipset and certain memory-related circuitry.
5V rail Powers USB ports/devices and some legacy drive interface logic, among other moderate-power components.
12V rail Powers the most demanding components in a modern system — the CPU, the GPU, drive spindle motors (on HDDs/optical drives), and case/cooling fans. In modern high-performance systems, the 12V rail typically carries the largest share of total system power draw by far, since CPUs and especially GPUs are the heaviest power consumers.

Exam Angle

Modern, demanding components — especially CPUs and GPUs — draw the overwhelming majority of their power from the 12V rail. If a question describes high-end graphics card installation requirements or overall system power delivery concerns, the 12V rail is almost always the relevant detail. The 3.3V and 5V rails matter conceptually (and for accurately identifying connector pinouts) but carry comparatively modest loads in a typical modern build.

CONCEPT 0320+4 Pin Motherboard Connector

The 20+4 pin connector (commonly called the main ATX power connector) is the primary connector delivering power from the PSU to the motherboard, supplying the various voltage rails discussed above to the board's power-distribution circuitry.

The "20+4" naming reflects its design: a 20-pin block plus a separate, detachable 4-pin block that can be connected alongside it to form a full 24-pin connector, or removed to leave a standalone 20-pin connector. This split design exists specifically for backward compatibility — older motherboards expecting only a 20-pin connector can still be powered by a PSU that splits off the extra 4 pins, while modern motherboards expecting the full 24-pin connector simply use both pieces together.

Why the Connector Splits Into 20 + 4
01
Older standard (20-pin). Early ATX motherboards used a single 20-pin main power connector, sufficient for the lower power demands of systems at the time.
02
Power demands increased. As CPUs, expansion cards, and overall systems became more power-hungry, the ATX standard was updated to add 4 additional pins, delivering more available power and additional ground connections.
03
Backward-compatible design. Rather than making old and new PSUs/motherboards entirely incompatible, the connector was designed so the extra 4 pins detach as their own small block — a 24-pin-capable PSU can power a 20-pin-only motherboard by simply not connecting (or physically separating) that extra block.

Not the Only Power Connector

While the 20+4 pin connector is the main motherboard power connector and the specific one named in this objective, real-world systems also typically require a separate 4-pin or 8-pin CPU/EPS power connector (delivering dedicated 12V power directly near the CPU socket, as referenced in objective 3.5), plus SATA power connectors for drives and PCIe power connectors for higher-power graphics cards. The 20+4 pin connector specifically addresses the motherboard's general power needs — it is not the sole power delivery point in a complete system.

CONCEPT 04Redundant Power Supply

A redundant power supply configuration uses two (or more) power supply units installed together in the same system, such that if one PSU fails, the other immediately and automatically continues supplying power without any interruption or downtime. This is a server/enterprise-class feature, almost never found in consumer desktops, and directly parallels the broader theme of redundancy seen elsewhere in the exam (RAID arrays in objective 3.4, redundant network links).

Why Servers Use Redundant PSUs

For a server running critical business applications, databases, or services that many users/systems depend on, an unplanned power supply failure causing a full outage can be extremely costly. A redundant PSU setup means a single failed unit doesn't take the whole system down — the surviving unit (or units) continue supplying power, often allowing the failed unit to be physically replaced (hot-swapped) without ever powering down the system at all, depending on the specific server hardware design.

Exam Angle

If a scenario emphasizes high availability, mission-critical uptime, or a server environment that "cannot go down," redundant power supplies are a strong candidate answer alongside other redundancy measures covered elsewhere in the exam (RAID, redundant network paths). This is purely an enterprise/server consideration — don't apply it to typical consumer desktop scenarios.

CONCEPT 05Modular Power Supply

A modular power supply allows individual cables to be physically connected or disconnected from the PSU itself, as opposed to having every cable permanently hardwired into the unit. This lets a technician install only the specific cables actually needed for a given build, rather than dealing with a fixed bundle of cables — some of which may go entirely unused.

TypeDescriptionTradeoff
Non-modularAll cables permanently hardwired/attached to the PSUSimplest, often least expensive; unused cables must be tucked away, hurting airflow/cable management
Semi-modularEssential cables (like the 20+4 pin and CPU power) are fixed; less commonly needed cables are detachableA middle ground between cost and cable management flexibility
Fully modularEvery cable is detachable, including the main motherboard and CPU power cablesBest cable management and airflow; typically the most expensive option

Why Modularity Matters in Practice

Beyond simple convenience, a fully or semi-modular PSU directly improves case airflow and cable management by eliminating unused cables that would otherwise need to be crammed somewhere inside the case, potentially blocking fans or restricting airflow. This is purely a build-quality-of-life and thermal consideration — modularity has no effect on the PSU's actual power output, wattage, or efficiency rating.

CONCEPT 06Wattage Rating

A PSU's wattage rating describes the maximum total power it can reliably deliver to all connected components simultaneously. Selecting a PSU with sufficient wattage for a given system's actual power demands is one of the most fundamental and practical decisions in any build or upgrade.

  • Undersized wattage — a PSU rated below the system's actual power draw can cause instability, random shutdowns or reboots under load, failure to boot at all, or in worst cases, premature PSU failure from being run consistently beyond its rated capacity.
  • Component power draw varies enormously — a high-end GPU alone can draw several hundred watts under load, while a basic office desktop with integrated graphics may need only a small fraction of that for the entire system.
  • Headroom matters — it's standard practice to select a PSU with some margin above the calculated/estimated total system power draw, both to maintain efficiency (PSUs are typically least efficient very close to their maximum rated load) and to leave room for future upgrades.
  • Upgrades change requirements — adding a more powerful GPU, additional drives, or more case fans to an existing system increases total power draw, and an older PSU that was perfectly adequate before the upgrade may become insufficient afterward.

Exam Angle

A classic scenario: "A user installs a new high-end graphics card, and the system now randomly shuts down or fails to boot under load." This points to insufficient PSU wattage for the new component's power demands — the troubleshooting answer is to verify the PSU's wattage rating against the new total system power requirements and upgrade the PSU if needed, not to assume the GPU itself is defective.

CONCEPT 07Energy Efficiency

A PSU's energy efficiency describes what percentage of the AC power it draws from the wall is actually converted into usable DC output power for components, versus how much is lost as waste heat during that conversion process. A more efficient PSU wastes less electricity as heat, reducing both electricity costs and the amount of heat the system's cooling has to deal with.

The most widely recognized efficiency standard is the 80 PLUS certification program, which tests and certifies PSUs at multiple tiers based on efficiency percentage at various load levels (typically tested at 20%, 50%, and 100% load).

80 PLUS Certification Tiers — Relative Efficiency

80 PLUS (base)≥80% efficiency at tested load points
80 PLUS BronzeImproved efficiency over base certification
80 PLUS SilverFurther improved efficiency
80 PLUS GoldHigh efficiency; common choice for performance builds
80 PLUS Platinum / TitaniumHighest efficiency tiers available

Why Efficiency Matters Beyond the Electric Bill

A less efficient PSU doesn't just waste more electricity — that wasted energy is released as heat inside the case, adding to the system's overall thermal load and requiring more cooling effort (covered in objective 3.5) to manage. In environments with many systems running continuously (server rooms, data centers, busy offices), efficiency differences compound significantly across electricity costs, cooling/HVAC demands, and equipment longevity over time.

Exam Angle

You don't need to memorize exact efficiency percentages for each 80 PLUS tier, but you should recognize the relative ranking — base 80 PLUS is the minimum, with Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum, and Titanium representing progressively higher efficiency — and understand that higher efficiency tiers generally cost more but reduce electricity waste and heat output, which matters more in continuously-running or high-power environments than in light, occasional-use systems.
